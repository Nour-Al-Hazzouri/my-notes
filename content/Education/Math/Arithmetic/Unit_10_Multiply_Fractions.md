# Unit 10: Multiply Fractions

## Overview
This unit focuses on multiplication of fractions, building upon students' understanding of fractions as parts of a whole and their knowledge of whole number multiplication. Multiplying fractions is a fundamental skill that extends students' computational abilities and provides tools for solving a wide range of real-world problems. This unit introduces various models and strategies for understanding and performing fraction multiplication.

## Key Concepts

### Concept 1: Multiplying Fractions by Whole Numbers
**Definition**: Multiplying a fraction by a whole number can be understood as taking multiple copies of that fraction or as multiplying the numerator by the whole number while keeping the denominator the same.

**Clarification**: When multiplying a fraction by a whole number, we can think of it as repeated addition of the fraction. Alternatively, we can multiply the numerator by the whole number and keep the denominator unchanged. This concept builds on students' understanding of multiplication as repeated addition.

**Example**: To calculate 3 × 2/5:
- Thinking of it as repeated addition: 2/5 + 2/5 + 2/5 = 6/5
- Using the algorithm: 3 × 2/5 = (3 × 2)/5 = 6/5 = 1 1/5

```
    Multiplying Fractions by Whole Numbers
    
    Example: 3 × 2/5 = 6/5 = 1 1/5
    
    Method 1: Repeated Addition
    
    2/5 + 2/5 + 2/5 = 6/5
    
    Visual representation:
    
    First 2/5:       Second 2/5:      Third 2/5:       Combined (6/5):
    ┌───┬───┬───┬───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┬───┬───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┬───┬───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┬───┬───┬───┐
    │///│///│   │   │   │ │///│///│   │   │   │ │///│///│   │   │   │ │///│///│///│///│///│
    └───┴───┴───┴───┴───┘ └───┴───┴───┴───┴───┘ └───┴───┴───┴───┴───┘ └───┴───┴───┴───┴───┘
                                                                      │///│   │   │   │   │
                                                                      └───┴───┴───┴───┴───┘
    
    Method 2: Multiply Numerator, Keep Denominator
    
    3 × 2/5 = (3 × 2)/5 = 6/5 = 1 1/5
    
    Rule: When multiplying a fraction by a whole number,
          multiply the numerator by the whole number and
          keep the denominator the same.
```

### Concept 2: Multiplying a Fraction by a Fraction
**Definition**: When multiplying two fractions, we multiply the numerators together and multiply the denominators together.

**Clarification**: Fraction multiplication can be visualized as finding a fraction of a fraction. The product of two fractions is often smaller than either factor, which is different from whole number multiplication where the product is always larger than the factors. This concept requires students to shift their understanding of what multiplication means.

**Example**: To multiply 2/3 × 3/4:
- Multiply the numerators: 2 × 3 = 6
- Multiply the denominators: 3 × 4 = 12
- The product is 6/12, which simplifies to 1/2

This can be visualized as finding 2/3 of 3/4, which is 1/2.

```
    Multiplying a Fraction by a Fraction
    
    Example: 2/3 × 3/4 = 6/12 = 1/2
    
    Step 1: Multiply the numerators
            2 × 3 = 6
    
    Step 2: Multiply the denominators
            3 × 4 = 12
    
    Step 3: Write the product and simplify if possible
            6/12 = 1/2
    
    Visual interpretation: Finding 2/3 of 3/4
    
    Start with 3/4:
    ┌───┬───┬───┬───┐
    │///│///│///│   │
    └───┴───┴───┴───┘
    
    Divide into 3 equal parts (for the denominator of 2/3):
    ┌───┬───┬───┬───┐
    │/│/│/│/│/│/│   │
    └─┴─┴─┴─┴─┴─┴───┘
     1   2   3
    
    Take 2 of those parts (for the numerator of 2/3):
    ┌───┬───┬───┬───┐
    │/│/│/│/│   │   │
    └─┴─┴─┴─┴───┴───┘
     1   2
    
    The result is 1/2 of the whole.
```

### Concept 3: Area Models for Fraction Multiplication
**Definition**: Area models represent fraction multiplication as finding the area of a rectangle with fractional side lengths.

**Clarification**: Area models provide a visual representation of fraction multiplication that helps students understand why we multiply numerators and denominators. The model shows how the unit square is divided into equal parts, with the product representing a specific portion of the whole.

**Example**: To visualize 2/3 × 3/4 using an area model:
1. Create a rectangle with width 2/3 and height 3/4
2. Divide the width into 3 equal parts and shade 2 parts
3. Divide the height into 4 equal parts and shade 3 parts
4. The rectangle is now divided into 12 equal parts (3 × 4), with 6 parts (2 × 3) shaded
5. The shaded area represents 6/12 = 1/2 of the whole

### Concept 4: Multiplying Mixed Numbers
**Definition**: Multiplying mixed numbers involves converting them to improper fractions first, then multiplying as usual.

**Clarification**: While it's possible to use the distributive property to multiply mixed numbers directly, converting to improper fractions first is usually more efficient and less prone to errors. This process reinforces the relationship between mixed numbers and improper fractions.

**Example**: To multiply 1 1/2 × 2 1/3:
1. Convert to improper fractions: 1 1/2 = 3/2 and 2 1/3 = 7/3
2. Multiply the fractions: 3/2 × 7/3 = 21/6 = 3 1/2
3. The product is 3 1/2

```
    Multiplying Mixed Numbers
    
    Example: 1 1/2 × 2 1/3 = 3 1/2
    
    Step 1: Convert mixed numbers to improper fractions
    
            1 1/2 = (1 × 2 + 1)/2 = 3/2
            2 1/3 = (2 × 3 + 1)/3 = 7/3
    
    Step 2: Multiply the improper fractions
    
            3/2 × 7/3 = (3 × 7)/(2 × 3) = 21/6
    
    Step 3: Simplify the result
    
            21/6 = 3 1/2
    
    Visual representation:
    
    1 1/2:                   2 1/3:
    ┌───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┐     ┌───┬───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┬───┐
    │///│///│ │///│   │     │///│///│///│ │///│///│///│ │///│   │   │
    └───┴───┘ └───┴───┘     └───┴───┴───┘ └───┴───┴───┘ └───┴───┴───┘
    
    Product (3 1/2):
    ┌───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┐ ┌───┬───┐
    │///│///│ │///│///│ │///│///│ │///│   │
    └───┴───┘ └───┴───┘ └───┴───┘ └───┴───┘
```

### Concept 5: Simplifying Before Multiplying
**Definition**: Simplifying before multiplying involves finding common factors in the numerator of one fraction and the denominator of another fraction, then dividing by these common factors before performing the multiplication.

**Clarification**: This strategy, also known as "canceling," makes calculations more efficient and helps avoid working with large numbers. It's based on the fact that dividing both the numerator and denominator by the same non-zero number doesn't change the value of a fraction.

**Example**: To multiply 3/8 × 4/9:
1. Identify that 3 and 9 have a common factor of 3
2. Simplify by dividing both by 3: (3 ÷ 3)/(8) × (4)/(9 ÷ 3) = 1/8 × 4/3
3. Multiply the simplified fractions: 1/8 × 4/3 = 4/24 = 1/6

```
    Simplifying Before Multiplying
    
    Example: 3/8 × 4/9 = 1/6
    
    Method 1: Multiply directly
    
    3/8 × 4/9 = (3 × 4)/(8 × 9) = 12/72 = 1/6
    
    Method 2: Simplify first, then multiply
    
    Step 1: Identify common factors
            3 and 9 have a common factor of 3
    
    Step 2: Simplify by dividing by common factors
    
            3/8 × 4/9 = (3÷3)/8 × 4/(9÷3) = 1/8 × 4/3
    
    Step 3: Multiply the simplified fractions
    
            1/8 × 4/3 = (1 × 4)/(8 × 3) = 4/24 = 1/6
    
    Visual representation:
    
    3/8 × 4/9 = ?
    
    ┌───┐
    │ 3 │   4
    ├───┤ × ─
    │ 8 │   9
    └───┘
    
    Simplify by dividing common factors:
    
    ┌───┐
    │ 1 │   4
    ├───┤ × ─
    │ 8 │   3
    └───┘
    
    Multiply:
    
    ┌───┐
    │ 4 │   1
    ├───┤ = ─
    │ 24│   6
    └───┘
```

### Concept 6: Word Problems with Fraction Multiplication
**Definition**: Word problems with fraction multiplication involve real-world scenarios where finding a fraction of a quantity or multiplying fractional measurements is required.

**Clarification**: These problems help students understand the practical applications of fraction multiplication. They often involve finding a part of a part, scaling recipes, calculating areas, or working with measurements. Interpreting the problem correctly is key to setting up the appropriate multiplication expression.

**Example**: If a recipe calls for 3/4 cup of flour, and you want to make 2/3 of the recipe, you need to find 2/3 of 3/4 cup of flour:
2/3 × 3/4 = 6/12 = 1/2 cup of flour

```
    Word Problems with Fraction Multiplication
    
    Example: Recipe Scaling
    
    Problem: A recipe calls for 3/4 cup of flour. If you want to make
             2/3 of the recipe, how much flour do you need?
    
    Solution: Find 2/3 of 3/4 cup of flour
    
              2/3 × 3/4 = (2 × 3)/(3 × 4) = 6/12 = 1/2 cup of flour
    
    Visual representation:
    
    Original amount (3/4 cup):
    ┌───┬───┬───┬───┐
    │///│///│///│   │
    └───┴───┴───┴───┘
    
    Divide into 3 equal parts (for the denominator of 2/3):
    ┌───┬───┬───┬───┐
    │/│/│/│/│/│/│   │
    └─┴─┴─┴─┴─┴─┴───┘
     1   2   3
    
    Take 2 of those parts (for the numerator of 2/3):
    ┌───┬───┬───┬───┐
    │/│/│/│/│   │   │
    └─┴─┴─┴─┴───┴───┘
     1   2
    
    The result is 1/2 cup of flour.
```

### Concept 7: Multiplying Fractions and Whole Numbers in Measurement Contexts
**Definition**: In measurement contexts, multiplying fractions and whole numbers involves finding fractional parts of units or scaling measurements.

**Clarification**: These applications connect fraction multiplication to real-world measurement scenarios, reinforcing the practical utility of this operation. Students learn to interpret products in terms of the appropriate units and to convert between different units when necessary.

**Example**: If one serving of cereal is 2/3 cup, how much cereal is needed for 5 servings?
5 × 2/3 = 10/3 = 3 1/3 cups of cereal

## Summary
Unit 10 develops students' understanding of fraction multiplication, building on their knowledge of fractions and whole number multiplication. Students learn to multiply fractions by whole numbers and by other fractions, use area models to visualize multiplication, work with mixed numbers, simplify before multiplying to make calculations more efficient, and solve word problems involving fraction multiplication in various contexts. These skills are essential for mathematical fluency and provide the foundation for more advanced operations with fractions and decimals.

## Connection to Next Unit
The multiplication skills developed in Unit 10 directly support Unit 11, which focuses on decimals and place value. Fractions and decimals are different representations of the same concept—parts of a whole—and many fractions can be expressed as decimals (e.g., 1/4 = 0.25). Understanding how to multiply fractions will help students work with decimal multiplication, as the underlying concepts are similar. Additionally, the area models used for fraction multiplication will be adapted for decimal multiplication, providing a visual representation that reinforces place value understanding.
