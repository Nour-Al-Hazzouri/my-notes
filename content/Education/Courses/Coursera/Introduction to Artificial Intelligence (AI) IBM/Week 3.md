# Week 3: AI Ethics and Governance

## Exploring Today's AI Concerns

### Responsible AI Development
- The hot topic in AI today is how to use it responsibly and address potential harmful consequences
- **IBM's 5 Pillars for Responsible AI**:
  - Explainability
  - Transparency
  - Robustness
  - Privacy
  - Fairness
- These pillars need to be considered throughout the entire AI lifecycle, not just initially
- Responsible AI development requires curiosity, asking questions, and teamwork

### Facial Recognition
- A major AI use case with 3 categories:
  1. **Facial Detection**: Identifying human faces
  2. **Facial Authentication**: 1:1 matching for user authentication
  3. **Facial Matching**: 1:many matching against a database
- **Dual Nature**:
  - Helpful uses: Security, user authentication
  - Concerning uses: Mass surveillance, violating privacy

### AI in Hiring and Workforce Management
- AI is being used for ranking job applicants
- Guardrails are needed to prevent bias based on sensitive attributes

### AI in Marketing
- Can micro-target ads effectively on social media
- Also enables misinformation
- Transparency and ethics principles are critical

### AI in Healthcare
- Used for disease prediction, care coordination, conversational agents
- Equitable care and trustworthiness are key requirements
- Common goals are unlocking data insights and improving patient experience

## Exploring AI and Ethics

### The Socio-Technical Challenge
- AI is pervasive in our lives and has significant social impact
- Raises ethical questions on design, development, deployment, and regulation
- Requires an inclusive effort involving:
  - Technical experts

  - Philosophers
  - Policymakers
  - Impacted communities

- Guidelines, principles, processes, and governance needed to ensure AI innovation is directed toward the desired future
- IBM views earning trust as critical, embedding ethics into the AI lifecycle through principled decision-making

### IBM's AI Ethics Principles
- AI should augment, not replace human intelligence
- Data/insights belong to creators
- AI must be transparent and explainable

### IBM's Pillars for Trustworthy AI
- Transparency to build trust
- Explainability and fairness
- Robustness through security and privacy

### Putting AI Ethics into Action
1. Building understanding and awareness
2. Establishing a governance structure to scale ethics practices
3. Operationalizing ethics into processes for:
   - Developers
   - Data scientists
   - Vendors

- Operationalization involves clarity on pillars like transparency, explainability, fairness, robustness, privacy for practical implementation

## Defining AI Ethics

### Ethics and Decision-Making
- Ethics guide human decision-making, especially decisions impacting others
- As AI automates/augments decision-making, it's critical AI is built with ethics at the core
- AI ethics explores maximizing AI's benefits while reducing risks/adverse impacts
- Covers data responsibility, privacy, inclusion, moral agency, value alignment, accountability, misuse

### 5 Pillars of AI Ethics

| Pillar | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| **Explainability** | AI can explain how/why it arrived at an outcome |
| **Fairness** | AI treats individuals/groups equitably, mitigating bias |
| **Robustness** | AI can handle exceptional conditions/adversarial attacks |
| **Transparency** | Appropriate information is shared on how AI was designed/developed |
| **Privacy** | AI prioritizes safeguarding privacy/data rights |

### Pillar Benefits
- **Explainability** allows AI to "show its work"
- **Fairness** helps AI counterbalance human biases
- **Robustness** allows AI to withstand intentional/unintentional interference
- **Transparency** provides information like training data, data collection, access
- **Privacy** means only minimum needed data is collected, never repurposed without consent

Together these 5 pillars enable designing, developing, deploying AI ethically and aligned with human expectations

## Understanding Bias and AI

### Defining Bias in AI
- Unwanted behaviors where AI systems used for consequential decisions systematically disadvantage certain groups or individuals

### Sources of Bias in AI
- Training data reflecting historical human bias/prejudice
- Imbalanced sampling (over/underrepresentation of groups)
- Data processing steps like feature engineering
- Framing/posing the problem incorrectly

### Impact Areas
- Healthcare
- Hiring
- Criminal justice

### Bias Mitigation Strategies
- Recognizing biases exist by having diverse teams
- Actively seeking out unbiased data sources
- Using technical approaches to constrain ML models and remove bias

### IBM's Approach
- AI Ethics Board focused on inclusive "Good Tech"
  - Researching ways to eliminate bias
  - Establishing standards and testing for bias
  - Leveraging AI for diversity & inclusion initiatives
- Uses AI for decision support while minimizing potential for bias
  - In HR for pay, hiring, promotion decisions
  - Assisting compliance and proactive bias reduction globally

## AI Ethics and Regulations

### Regulatory Landscape
- Regulation is a government rule enforceable by law
- AI regulations landscape is evolving rapidly
- Understanding AI regulations is important for designing, developing, deploying and using AI legally/ethically

### IBM's Regulatory Approach
- Calls for "precision regulation" of AI
- Supports policies increasing responsibilities for developing trustworthy AI
- Precision regulation refers to risk-based, context-specific regulation
- Allocates responsibility to party closest to the risk throughout AI lifecycle

### IBM's Proposed Framework
**Five Policy Imperatives:**
1. Designate an AI ethics official for compliance
2. Develop different rules for different risk levels (regulate AI context, not technology itself)
3. Don't hide your AI, make it transparent
4. Make AI explainable, not a "black box"
5. Test AI for bias

## AI Ethics, Governance, and ESG

### AI Governance
- Organization's act of governing through instructions, processes, systems to direct, evaluate, monitor AI lifecycle
- Objective is delivering trustworthy AI by establishing accountability, responsibility, oversight
- Benefits include trust, efficiency, compliance

### Governance Program Components
- **People**: Defined roles/responsibilities for building/managing trustworthy AI
- **Processes**: For building, managing, monitoring, communicating about AI
- **Tools**: For visibility and consistency into AI system performance

### ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance)
- Measures non-financial risks and opportunities at companies
- **IBM's ESG Strategy** = "IBM Impact" with 3 pillars:
  1. Environmental impact
  2. Equitable impact
  3. Ethical impact
- Aims to make positive impact through business ethics, environment, communities

### Governance Aspect of ESG
- Creating innovations, policies, practices prioritizing ethics, trust, transparency, accountability
- AI ethics is a significant governance initiative
- Goal of training 1,000 ecosystem partners in tech ethics by 2022
