# Unit 3: Intro to Division

## Overview
This unit introduces the fundamental concept of division, which is essential for solving problems involving equal sharing and grouping. Division is presented as the inverse operation of multiplication, building upon students' prior knowledge. Understanding division is crucial for developing number sense, solving real-world problems, and establishing a foundation for more advanced mathematical concepts like fractions and algebraic thinking.

## Key Concepts

### Concept 1: Division as Equal Groups
**Definition**: Division represents sharing a quantity into equal groups or determining how many equal groups can be formed from a given quantity.

**Clarification**: There are two interpretations of division: partitive division (sharing equally) and quotative division (measuring). In partitive division, we know the number of groups and need to find how many items are in each group. In quotative division, we know the size of each group and need to find how many groups can be formed.

**Example**: If 12 cookies are shared equally among 3 children (partitive division), each child gets 12 ÷ 3 = 4 cookies. If we need to package 12 cookies into bags of 4 cookies each (quotative division), we can make 12 ÷ 4 = 3 bags.

```
    Division as Equal Groups
    
    Partitive Division (Sharing):
    12 cookies ÷ 3 children = 4 cookies per child
    
    Child 1    Child 2    Child 3
    🍪🍪🍪🍪    🍪🍪🍪🍪    🍪🍪🍪🍪
    
    Quotative Division (Grouping):
    12 cookies ÷ 4 cookies per bag = 3 bags
    
    Bag 1      Bag 2      Bag 3
    🍪🍪🍪🍪    🍪🍪🍪🍪    🍪🍪🍪🍪
```

### Concept 2: Division with Arrays
**Definition**: Arrays can be used to visualize division by arranging objects in equal rows or columns.

**Clarification**: When dividing using arrays, we either determine how many objects are in each row (when the number of rows is known) or how many rows can be formed (when the number of objects per row is known). This visual representation helps students understand the connection between multiplication and division.

**Example**: An array of 15 objects arranged in 3 rows shows that 15 ÷ 3 = 5 objects per row. Alternatively, if we arrange 15 objects with 5 objects per row, we can form 15 ÷ 5 = 3 rows.

```
    Division with Arrays
    
    15 ÷ 3 = 5 (objects per row)
    
    Row 1: * * * * *
    Row 2: * * * * *
    Row 3: * * * * *
    
    15 ÷ 5 = 3 (rows)
    
    Row 1: * * * * *
    Row 2: * * * * *
    Row 3: * * * * *
```

### Concept 3: Relationship Between Division and Multiplication
**Definition**: Division and multiplication are inverse operations, meaning they undo each other.

**Clarification**: If a ÷ b = c, then a = b × c. This relationship allows students to use their knowledge of multiplication facts to solve division problems. Understanding this connection is crucial for developing fluency with division.

**Example**: To solve 24 ÷ 6 = ?, students can think "6 times what number equals 24?" Since 6 × 4 = 24, then 24 ÷ 6 = 4.

```
    Division and Multiplication Relationship
    
    Division:      24 ÷ 6 = 4
    
    Multiplication: 6 × 4 = 24
    
    Think: "6 times what number equals 24?"
    
    +---+---+---+---+
    | 6 | 6 | 6 | 6 |  → 4 groups of 6 = 24
    +---+---+---+---+
    
    Therefore: 24 ÷ 6 = 4
```

### Concept 4: Division Fact Families
**Definition**: A fact family consists of related multiplication and division facts using the same three numbers.

**Clarification**: For any three numbers a, b, and c, where a = b × c, there are two multiplication facts (b × c = a and c × b = a) and two division facts (a ÷ b = c and a ÷ c = b). Understanding fact families helps students see the interconnection between multiplication and division.

**Example**: The fact family for 3, 5, and 15 includes: 3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, and 15 ÷ 5 = 3.

```
    Division Fact Families
    
    Fact Family for 3, 5, and 15:
    
    Multiplication Facts:
    3 × 5 = 15
    5 × 3 = 15
    
    Division Facts:
    15 ÷ 3 = 5
    15 ÷ 5 = 3
    
    Visual representation:
    
    3 groups of 5:
    [5][5][5] = 15
    
    5 groups of 3:
    [3][3][3][3][3] = 15
    
    15 divided into groups of 3:
    [3][3][3][3][3] = 5 groups
    
    15 divided into groups of 5:
    [5][5][5] = 3 groups
```

### Concept 5: Division by 1
**Definition**: When a number is divided by 1, the quotient is the number itself.

**Clarification**: Division by 1 is a special case where the number remains unchanged. This is because any number contains exactly that many groups of 1.

**Example**: 7 ÷ 1 = 7 because there are 7 groups of 1 in 7.

```
    Division by 1
    
    7 ÷ 1 = 7
    
    [1][1][1][1][1][1][1]
     ↑  ↑  ↑  ↑  ↑  ↑  ↑
     1  2  3  4  5  6  7
    
    There are 7 groups of 1 in 7
    
    Special property:
    Any number divided by 1 equals that number
    n ÷ 1 = n
```

### Concept 6: Division by 2, 5, and 10
**Definition**: Division by 2, 5, and 10 follows specific patterns that can be recognized and applied to quickly determine quotients.

**Clarification**: These division facts are often easier to learn because they relate to familiar patterns: halving for division by 2, counting by 5s for division by 5, and working with place value for division by 10.

**Example**: 
- For division by 2: 14 ÷ 2 = 7 (half of 14)
- For division by 5: 35 ÷ 5 = 7 (counting by 5s: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35 → 7 groups)
- For division by 10: 70 ÷ 10 = 7 (remove the zero from the end)

```
    Division by 2, 5, and 10
    
    Division by 2 (Halving):
    14 ÷ 2 = 7
    
    [*][*] [*][*] [*][*] [*][*] [*][*] [*][*] [*][*]
      1      2      3      4      5      6      7
    
    Division by 5 (Counting by 5s):
    35 ÷ 5 = 7
    
    [5] [5] [5] [5] [5] [5] [5]
     ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓
     5  10  15  20  25  30  35
     1   2   3   4   5   6   7 groups
    
    Division by 10 (Place Value):
    70 ÷ 10 = 7
    
    Remove the zero: 70 → 7
```

### Concept 7: Division by 3, 6, and 9
**Definition**: Division facts involving 3, 6, and 9 can be approached using specific strategies based on patterns and relationships.

**Clarification**: These division facts can be more challenging but can be mastered through understanding the relationship with multiplication and recognizing patterns.

**Example**: 
- For division by 3: 27 ÷ 3 = 9 (since 3 × 9 = 27)
- For division by 6: 42 ÷ 6 = 7 (since 6 × 7 = 42)
- For division by 9: 81 ÷ 9 = 9 (since 9 × 9 = 81)

```
    Division by 3, 6, and 9
    
    Division by 3:
    27 ÷ 3 = 9
    
    [3] [3] [3] [3] [3] [3] [3] [3] [3]
     ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓
     3   6   9  12  15  18  21  24  27
     1   2   3   4   5   6   7   8   9 groups
    
    Division by 6:
    42 ÷ 6 = 7
    
    [6] [6] [6] [6] [6] [6] [6]
     ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓
     6  12  18  24  30  36  42
     1   2   3   4   5   6   7 groups
    
    Division by 9:
    81 ÷ 9 = 9
    
    [9] [9] [9] [9] [9] [9] [9] [9] [9]
     ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓   ↓
     9  18  27  36  45  54  63  72  81
     1   2   3   4   5   6   7   8   9 groups
```

## Summary
Unit 3 introduces division as both equal sharing and determining how many equal groups can be formed. Students learn to visualize division using arrays and understand the inverse relationship between multiplication and division through fact families. The unit covers special cases like division by 1 and strategies for dividing by 2, 5, 10, 3, 6, and 9. By the end of this unit, students should be able to interpret division situations, use visual models to represent division, and solve basic division problems using their knowledge of multiplication facts.

## Connection to Next Unit
The division concepts introduced in Unit 3 provide a foundation for Unit 4, which focuses on understanding fractions. Division naturally leads to fractions when the dividend is not a multiple of the divisor, resulting in a remainder. Understanding how to divide objects into equal groups directly connects to the concept of fractions as equal parts of a whole. The relationship between division and fractions will be explored further as students develop their understanding of fractional parts and their representations.
